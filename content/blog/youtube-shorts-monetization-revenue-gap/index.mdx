## Shorts Revenue Was a Bad Joke. It Is Starting to Not Be.

When YouTube replaced the Shorts Fund with a revenue-sharing model in early 2023, the RPMs were painful. Creators were earning fractions of a cent per thousand views. A video with 5 million Shorts views might generate less than $50. Long-form creators watched Shorts numbers with bewilderment — massive view counts, essentially no revenue.

That math has been changing. And in July 2024, YouTube made the shift explicit: the company confirmed it is "closing the gap" between Shorts and long-form monetization as Shorts advertising inventory matures.

The current numbers are still not parity with long-form. They never will be — the formats are structurally different and serve different advertiser use cases. But the trajectory matters enormously for content strategy, and the implications for how to allocate production resources across formats are now substantial enough to be worth modeling explicitly.

We have been tracking Shorts RPM data across our portfolio since the revenue-share model launched. Here is what the numbers actually show.

## The Baseline: Where Shorts RPM Started

The Shorts Fund model that YouTube operated in 2022 was essentially a bonus pool distributed based on viewership milestones. The payouts had no relationship to advertising economics. A creator with 10 million Shorts views might receive $500 from the fund. Another might receive $1,200. The inconsistency was intentional — it was a discretionary rewards pool, not a genuine monetization system.

When YouTube moved to revenue sharing in February 2023, the theoretical model improved. Creators began earning 45% of the ad revenue allocated to Shorts. But the advertiser demand for Shorts inventory in early 2023 was thin. Ad loads were limited. CPMs were low. The result was RPMs hovering between $0.03 and $0.06 per thousand views for most channels.

For context: typical long-form RPMs in competitive niches ranged from $3 to $15+ per thousand views at the same time. The gap was not a gap — it was a chasm.

## The 2024 Inflection Point

By April 2024, Shorts RPMs had climbed to above $0.10 per thousand views on YouTube's own disclosure. In several niches — personal finance, business, technology — Hype On clients were seeing Shorts RPMs between $0.12 and $0.18 per thousand views. Still dramatically below long-form. But the trajectory had become recognizable.

The mechanism driving improvement is simple: advertiser adoption. Shorts ad formats — particularly the full-screen, sound-on vertical video format — have proven measurably effective for certain campaign types. Direct response campaigns targeting mobile-first audiences have found Shorts inventory particularly valuable. As more advertisers compete for that inventory, CPMs rise. As CPMs rise, the 45% creator share translates into meaningful RPM improvement.

YouTube confirmed in July 2024 that Shorts RPM had tripled over the previous 18 months. The "closing the gap" language was not hype — it reflected a real structural improvement in how the platform monetizes vertical content.

## The Real Numbers: What Our Data Shows

Across our portfolio of channels in mid-2024, Shorts RPM distribution breaks down roughly as follows:

**Low RPM Shorts (<$0.08):** Channels where Shorts content is entertainment-focused with broad demographic reach, or channels in niches with weak advertiser demand (gaming, memes, general entertainment). These channels generate Shorts views cheaply but the revenue per view remains marginal.

**Mid RPM Shorts ($0.08-$0.15):** The majority of channels in professionally produced niches. This range includes lifestyle, fitness, food, and general business content. At this RPM, a Shorts video with 1 million views generates $80-$150. Still not long-form comparable, but meaningful at scale.

**High RPM Shorts ($0.15-$0.30+):** Business, finance, SaaS, career, and B2B-adjacent content. These niches have the highest advertiser demand on the platform in any format. Shorts in these categories are now approaching 10% of long-form RPMs in the same niche — which changes the production calculus considerably.

The implication for content strategy: Shorts in high-CPM niches are now financially viable as a standalone component of the revenue mix, not just a growth channel feeding long-form.

## The Hybrid Strategy: Why Long-Form Still Wins

The improved Shorts monetization does not displace long-form. The economics remain fundamentally different, and the audiences served by each format are distinct.

Long-form content builds the relationship that converts viewers into subscribers, community members, and ultimately buyers of any product or service the channel connects to. Long-form watch time is the primary driver of algorithmic recommendation trust. The RPM differential — even with Shorts improving — means that a 10-minute video generating 500,000 views will produce dramatically more AdSense revenue than a 60-second Short generating the same views.

What Shorts enable is discovery at a scale and cost that long-form cannot match. A Shorts video costs a fraction of a long-form video to produce and can reach audiences who have never encountered the channel. When those viewers migrate to long-form, the economics shift entirely in favor of the creator.

Our hybrid content modeling for clients builds this explicitly: Shorts are the acquisition channel, long-form is the retention and monetization channel. The revenue model accounts for both the direct Shorts RPM and the indirect contribution of Shorts-driven viewers to long-form watch time and subscriber growth.

## What This Means for Production Allocation

For channels currently producing only long-form content, the question is not whether to add Shorts — it is how to add Shorts without compromising long-form quality. The answer is efficient repurposing.

Long-form content contains natural 30-90 second segments that perform well as Shorts: the most striking claim in the video, the key insight stated plainly, the demonstration moment, the surprising reveal. Identifying and extracting these segments requires skill — it is not simple clip cutting — but it is fundamentally more efficient than producing Shorts from scratch.

Hype On's production pipeline includes a dedicated Shorts extraction process for every long-form video. The result is 3-5 Shorts per long-form video, each optimized for vertical format, with appropriate hooks and format adaptations. The additional production cost is less than 15% of the base video cost. The reach multiplier justifies that cost reliably.

The channels in our portfolio that implement this hybrid model generate 60-80% more total channel views than comparable long-form-only channels. With Shorts RPMs continuing to improve, that view difference is increasingly translating into revenue difference.

## What We Predicted — And the Trajectory Through 2025

We built our hybrid strategy model for clients in 2023 on the assumption that Shorts RPM would continue improving as advertiser adoption grew. The July 2024 announcement confirmed the trajectory.

By 2025, top Shorts creators in high-CPM niches were reporting RPMs above $0.15 on a consistent basis. In the finance and business categories, several channels in our portfolio crossed $0.20. The gap between Shorts and long-form remains large — it will always remain large for structural reasons — but the Shorts contribution to total channel revenue has grown from negligible to meaningful for most professionally produced channels.

The channels that ignored Shorts until 2024 are now playing catch-up, not just on reach, but on the monetization learning curve. The creators who built Shorts operations in 2023, when the revenue was thin, have now run enough tests to understand what performs in their niche. That institutional knowledge compounds.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the current YouTube Shorts RPM in 2024?

As of mid-2024, YouTube Shorts RPMs range broadly from $0.03 to $0.30+ per thousand views depending on content category and audience. YouTube confirmed RPMs have tripled over the previous 18 months. Business, finance, and B2B niches see the highest Shorts RPMs, often $0.15-$0.20+. Entertainment and gaming niches remain at the lower end.

### How does Shorts monetization work compared to long-form?

Creators earn 45% of the ad revenue allocated to Shorts content they produce. YouTube aggregates ad revenue from Shorts viewing sessions and distributes the creator share proportionally based on watch time. This differs from long-form, where ad revenue is attributed directly to individual video views. The structural difference is why Shorts RPMs remain below long-form even as the gap narrows.

### Should I focus on Shorts or long-form for revenue?

Long-form remains the higher RPM format by a significant margin and should remain the foundation of a revenue-focused content strategy. Shorts play a complementary role: discovery and audience acquisition at low production cost, with an improving but still secondary monetization contribution. The optimal strategy is hybrid — Shorts for reach, long-form for depth and primary revenue.

### How many Shorts views do you need to make meaningful revenue?

At mid-2024 RPMs ($0.10-$0.15 average), a channel would need 10 million Shorts views per month to generate $1,000-$1,500 in direct Shorts revenue. For context, the same 10 million views in long-form at $3 RPM would generate $30,000. Shorts revenue is meaningful as a supplemental stream at scale, not as a standalone monetization model.

### Will Shorts RPMs ever reach parity with long-form?

No — the formats are structurally different. Long-form videos accommodate pre-roll, mid-roll, and display ads, with higher advertiser willingness to pay for attentive, longer viewing sessions. Shorts have more limited ad inventory and shorter average viewing windows. RPM parity is not a realistic target. However, Shorts RPMs will continue improving as advertiser demand for vertical video inventory grows globally.
